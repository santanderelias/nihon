The basic sentence structure in Japanese is Subject - Object - Verb (SOV).

Example: 私はリンゴを食べます (Watashi wa ringo o tabemasu) - I eat an apple.
  • 私 (Watashi) - I (Subject)
  • リンゴ (ringo) - apple (Object)
  • を食べます (o tabemasu) - eat (Verb)

Particles are short words that indicate the function of a noun in a sentence.

  • は (wa): Marks the topic of the sentence. (Pronounced 'wa')
  • が (ga): Marks the subject of the sentence.
  • を (o): Marks the direct object. (Pronounced 'o')
  • に (ni): Indicates location (existence), destination, or a specific time.
  • で (de): Indicates the location of an action.

Japanese verbs are conjugated based on their group. Here's a basic overview of the polite (ます/masu) form.

Group 1 (u-verbs)
Example: 読む (yomu) - to read -> 読みます (yomimasu) - (I) read.
Group 2 (ru-verbs)
Example: 食べる (taberu) - to eat -> 食べます (tabemasu) - (I) eat.
Group 3 (Irregular verbs)
Example: する (suru) - to do -> します (shimasu) - (I) do.

Adjectives ending in い (i) are called i-adjectives. They can be conjugated.

Present: 新しい (atarashii) - new
Negative: 新しくない (atarashikunai) - not new
Past: 新しかった (atarashikatta) - was new

Adjectives that require な (na) before a noun are na-adjectives. They use です (desu) for conjugation.

Present: きれい (kirei) -> きれいです (kirei desu) - pretty
Negative: きれいじゃないです (kirei janai desu) - not pretty
Past: きれいでした (kirei deshita) - was pretty
